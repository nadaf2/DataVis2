Despite making up 40% of the workforce and having a targeted 2020 objective of 30%, women in Japan still only hold 15% of managerial positions in 2021.

Women made up slightly more than a third (35.6%) of managers in Canada, although men still hold over 90% of C-Level Executive positions.

Australia has the highest representation of Women Managers in the Asia-Pacific. Yet women are less likely to reach the top levels of management and fall behind men by 18 percent.

Despite having a 36% higher chance of being hired than males, women are much less likely to view a job and subsequently apply. The extremely gendered perception of women's "natural" position as primary caretakers is one of the things that appears to impede progress towards gender equality. Such norms are particularly expensive for women, who end up paying a significant "care penalty". This obligation compounds the difficulties faced by women by taking time out of paid work and then negotiating a return.
